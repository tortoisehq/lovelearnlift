It started as a panic. *"The build failed,"* I said. It hadn't. What had actually happened was quieter, and worse: the design I'd spent a day on existed only as a folder of mockups — beautiful, finished-looking, and wired into nothing. The thing I thought I'd built wasn't real, because it lived nowhere a thumb could reach it.

So I spent a day wiring it in for real, into the phone app. I held the shape and handed the repetitive parts to a fleet of agents working in parallel: the theme, the screens, dark mode, a fingerprint login so I could stop typing a password to dogfood my own app. By evening it was on my phone, updating live every few minutes without a single trip through an app store.

## It Was Never a Health App

And then, living with it, the framing I'd started with went hollow. I'd been calling it a health tracker. But a tracker is a silo — this app for your sleep, that one for your money, another for your mood — and a silo can only ever tell you about itself.

The unfair thing isn't any one signal. It's crossing them. Your worst-sleep weeks are your highest-spend weeks. Your best work follows the mornings you trained. No single-purpose app can see that, because seeing it needs your whole life in one place. That's not a feature you bolt onto a health app. It's a different product — a self-improvement platform, where the patterns *are* the product. You don't open it to log a thing; you open it to find out what your body and your words and your days quietly revealed.
