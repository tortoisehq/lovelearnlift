Some build days don't make a screenshot.

Today, Steward, the voice-first tool that turns a wrecked railroader's spoken account into a filed grievance, got almost nothing you could point a camera at. One small new screen. That's the whole visible change.

![A single locomotive resting in a roundhouse bay at dusk, warm work-light spilling out the arched door.](./steward-s19-frame-before-drywall.svg)

Underneath was the real day. We pushed the live database the rest of the way over the line: deadline tracking, evidence storage, and a hard ceiling on cost. Then we proved the cost ceiling actually holds, instead of trusting that it would. We sat inside the union's own grievance system and learned exactly how a real grievance is shaped. We checked our filing-deadline math against years of real records. It matched.

Then we built the boring, beautiful thing: a way to capture every task so clearly that anyone, a stranger or a tired version of myself at 2 a.m., can pick it up cold and know what got done, and why.

None of that demos. All of it is the frame the rest of the house hangs on.

## Probably Fine Isn't a Standard

The review caught two real mistakes before they shipped. The build refused to fake "done." When you're handling another person's grievance, "probably fine" isn't a standard, and a tool that fakes "done" is worse than no tool at all.

Tomorrow we hang drywall. Today we made the frame square.

Lay the frame. Then build.
